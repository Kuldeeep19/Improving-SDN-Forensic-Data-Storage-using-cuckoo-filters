================================================================
  CHAPTER 1: THE REAL-WORLD STORY (Why this project exists)
================================================================

Imagine a big office building with 500 employees. Every day,
thousands of people walk in and out through different doors.
The building has security cameras at every door.

One day, someone breaks into the server room and steals
important files. The police come and say: "Show us the
security footage from 3 days ago so we can find who did it."

Now here's the problem:
  - The cameras record 24/7, producing HUGE amounts of video.
  - The hard disk can only store 2 days of footage.
  - Every 2 days, the old footage is AUTOMATICALLY DELETED
    to make room for new footage.

So when the police ask for footage from 3 days ago...
it's GONE. Permanently deleted. The criminal gets away.

THIS IS EXACTLY THE PROBLEM IN COMPUTER NETWORKS.

Instead of a building, think of the INTERNET.
Instead of people walking through doors, think of DATA PACKETS
flowing through ROUTERS (the internet's traffic junctions).
Instead of security cameras, think of LOG FILES that record
which packets passed through which router.

When a hacker attacks a company, the security team needs to
look at old logs to trace WHERE the attack came from. But if
the logs were deleted because the storage was full...
the hacker gets away. Evidence destroyed.

Our project fixes this problem.


================================================================
  CHAPTER 2: KEY WORDS EXPLAINED (Dictionary)
================================================================

Before we go further, let's define every technical word you'll
see in this project. Think of this as a dictionary.

--- PACKET ---
When you send a message on WhatsApp, your phone doesn't send
the whole message as one piece. It breaks it into small chunks
called "packets". Each packet travels through the internet
independently, bouncing from router to router, until it
reaches your friend's phone and gets reassembled.

Think of it like sending a book by post: instead of mailing
the whole book, you tear out each page and mail them separately.
Each page is a "packet".

--- ROUTER ---
A router is like a traffic junction on a highway. When a packet
arrives, the router looks at the destination address and decides
which road to send it on next. Your home WiFi box is a simple
router. The internet has millions of them.

--- NETWORK ---
A network is just a bunch of computers and routers connected
together. Your home WiFi is a small network. The internet is
the biggest network in the world.

--- SDN (Software Defined Networking) ---
In a normal network, each router makes its own decisions about
where to send packets. In an SDN, there is ONE CENTRAL BRAIN
called the "Controller" that tells ALL routers what to do.

Think of it like traffic:
  - Normal network = each traffic junction has its own traffic
    cop making independent decisions.
  - SDN = there's ONE traffic control room that sees ALL
    junctions on a big screen and tells every cop exactly
    what to do.

This central control makes SDN very powerful and is used in
modern data centers (Google, Amazon, Microsoft all use SDN).

--- FORENSICS ---
Forensics means investigating AFTER something bad happened.
Like how police examine a crime scene for fingerprints and
evidence. "Network forensics" means examining network logs
after a cyber attack to find out WHO did it, HOW they got in,
and WHICH path through the network they used.

--- TRACEABILITY ---
Traceability means: "Can we still find the evidence?"

If a hacker attacked 3 days ago, and we can still find the
record of their attack in our logs, that's good traceability.
If the logs were deleted, traceability = 0%. Evidence is gone.

--- HASH FUNCTION ---
A hash function is like a magic blender. You put in any input
(like a name, a number, or a whole document), and it spits
out a fixed-size number.

Example:
  hash("Kuldeep")    = 47382
  hash("Atharva")    = 91205
  hash("Hello World") = 83741

Key properties:
  1. Same input ALWAYS gives same output.
  2. Different inputs USUALLY give different outputs.
  3. You can't reverse it (you can't get "Kuldeep" back from
     47382).
  4. The output is always a fixed size (e.g., always a number
     between 0 and 99999).

We use a specific hash function called "MurmurHash" (mmh3) in
our project. The name sounds funny but it's very fast and
reliable.

--- FINGERPRINT ---
In this project, a "fingerprint" is a short code generated
from a packet's data using a hash function. Instead of storing
the entire packet (which could be huge), we store just its
fingerprint (a tiny number). Later, when we want to check if
a specific packet passed through, we compute its fingerprint
and check if that fingerprint exists in our storage.

It's like storing a person's thumbprint instead of their
entire photo album. Much smaller, but still identifies them.

--- FALSE POSITIVE ---
Sometimes, two completely different packets can produce the
SAME fingerprint (just like two different people could
theoretically have similar thumbprints). When this happens,
our system might say "Yes, this packet passed through!" even
though it didn't. This wrong "Yes" is called a false positive.

A good system keeps false positives very rare (below 1%).

--- FILTER (Data Structure) ---
A "filter" in computer science is a tool that answers one
question very quickly: "Have I seen this item before?"

You put items IN, and later you can ASK: "Is this item here?"
The filter says YES or NO.

The two types of filters in our project:
  1. Bloom Filter (the old, flawed one)
  2. Cuckoo Filter (our improved replacement)

--- BLOOM FILTER ---
Invented by Burton Bloom in 1970. It's a long row of bits
(0s and 1s), like a row of light switches.

How it works:
  1. Start with all switches OFF (all 0s).
  2. To INSERT an item: run it through several hash functions.
     Each hash gives a position number. Flip those switches ON.
  3. To CHECK if an item exists: run it through the same hash
     functions. Check those positions. If ALL are ON, say "YES,
     probably here". If ANY are OFF, say "NO, definitely not".

The GOOD:
  - Extremely small (1024 GB of logs -> 52 MB Bloom Filter).
  - Very fast.

The BAD:
  - CANNOT DELETE individual items. The switches are shared
    between multiple items. If you turn off a switch to remove
    one item, you might accidentally remove other items too.
  - When it fills up, you must WIPE EVERYTHING and start over.
    ALL evidence is destroyed instantly.

--- CUCKOO FILTER ---
Named after the cuckoo bird. Why? Because cuckoo birds push
other birds' eggs out of their nests to make room for their own.
Similarly, in a Cuckoo Filter, when a spot is full, the new
item KICKS OUT the existing item and takes its place. The kicked
item then goes to find another spot.

How it works:
  1. Each item gets a FINGERPRINT (a small number).
  2. Each item has TWO possible spots where it can live
     (calculated using hash functions).
  3. To INSERT: try spot 1. If full, try spot 2. If both full,
     KICK OUT someone from spot 1, put yourself there, and
     the kicked item goes to ITS alternate spot. This chain
     of kicks continues until everyone finds a home.
  4. To CHECK: look at both possible spots. If the fingerprint
     is there, say "YES".
  5. To DELETE: find the fingerprint in one of its two spots
     and remove it. DONE. No side effects.

The KEY ADVANTAGE:
  - CAN delete individual items! This is impossible with Bloom.
  - Same small size as Bloom Filter.
  - Actually has a LOWER false positive rate than Bloom!

--- SLIDING WINDOW ---
Imagine you have a notebook with 100 pages. You keep writing
police reports, one per page. When you fill page 100, you need
space. Instead of burning the whole notebook (that's what
Bloom does!), you carefully tear out just the 10 OLDEST pages
of routine reports, keeping all the important criminal case
pages. That's a "sliding window" -- it slides forward in time,
removing only the oldest, least important entries.

--- ATTACK-AWARE ---
Our sliding window is SMART. It knows which entries are from
normal traffic (benign) and which are from attacks. It ONLY
deletes old normal traffic. It NEVER touches attack evidence.
That's why it's "attack-aware".

--- SNAPSHOT ---
A snapshot is like taking a photograph of your notebook before
you tear out any pages. You save the photo permanently, and
then start a fresh notebook. The old evidence lives forever
in the photo (on disk). Nothing is deleted. Ever.

--- IMMUTABLE ---
Immutable means "cannot be changed". Once a snapshot is saved,
nobody can modify it. Like a printed photograph vs. a digital
photo -- you can't edit a printed photo. This is important for
legal evidence: in court, the police need to prove that
evidence was never tampered with.

--- TOPOLOGY ---
Topology means "the shape of the network" -- how the routers
are connected to each other.

  Linear topology: routers in a straight line
    S0 -- S1 -- S2 -- S3 -- S4 -- S5

  Tree topology: routers in a branching tree (like a family tree)
              S0
             /  \
           S1    S2
          / \   / \
        S3  S4 S5  S6

Real networks use tree topologies. Our project tests both.

--- SPIE (Source Path Isolation Engine) ---
A method to find which routers a packet passed through.
The idea: ask EVERY router "Did you see this packet?" and
collect all the "YES" answers. The problem: you have to ask
EVERY router, even the ones that never saw the packet. Slow.

--- PARENT POINTER ---
Our improved method. Instead of asking every router, each
router remembers WHO gave it the packet (its "parent"). So
to trace a path, you just ask the last router: "Who gave
you this packet?" It says "Router 5". You ask Router 5:
"Who gave YOU this packet?" It says "Router 3". And so on,
walking backward until you reach the attacker. Much faster
-- you only visit routers that were actually on the path.

--- SDN CONTROLLER ---
The "brain" of an SDN network. It sits above all the switches
and has a bird's-eye view of the entire network. In our project,
the controller is the one that runs forensic investigations:
it asks switches about packets and reconstructs attack paths.

--- CIC-IDS2017 DATASET ---
A real-world dataset created by the Canadian Institute for
Cybersecurity. They set up a network with 12 computers and
one attacker (using Kali Linux, a hacking tool). Over 5 days,
they captured all network traffic -- both normal browsing AND
actual attacks (brute-force password guessing, DDoS, web
attacks, etc.). We use the Tuesday file which has FTP-Patator
brute-force attacks mixed with normal traffic.


================================================================
  CHAPTER 3: THE PROBLEM (What's broken and why)
================================================================

Two researchers at our university (IIIT Hyderabad) worked on
this problem before us:

PAPER 1: Varun Sharma (our guide's student)
  He showed that Bloom Filters can compress 1024 GB of network
  logs down to just 52 MB. Amazing compression! He tested it
  on a simulated network with 6 routers.

  BUT: At the end of his paper, he wrote: "In future, we should
  try Cuckoo Filters." He knew Bloom Filters had the deletion
  problem but didn't fix it.

PAPER 2: Shesha Shila Bharadwaj
  She showed how to trace an attacker's path by querying Bloom
  Filters at each router. She introduced the concept of
  "traceability" -- can you find the evidence later?

  BUT: Her method queries EVERY router (slow) and doesn't
  preserve the ORDER of the path.

THE THREE PROBLEMS WE INHERITED:

  Problem 1: EVIDENCE DESTRUCTION
    When a Bloom Filter fills up (which happens regularly with
    high-speed network traffic), it wipes ALL stored evidence.
    In our experiments, this happened 4 times in just 50,000
    packets. After the wipes, only 2% of attack evidence
    remained. That's a 98% evidence loss. Useless for forensics.

  Problem 2: NO SELECTIVE DELETION
    Privacy laws (like GDPR in Europe) sometimes require you
    to delete a SPECIFIC user's data on request. With Bloom
    Filters, the only option is to delete EVERYTHING. You can't
    target one person's data without destroying everyone else's.

  Problem 3: SLOW AND UNORDERED TRACEBACK
    The existing traceback method (SPIE) asks EVERY router in
    the network "Did you see this packet?" This gives you a
    LIST of routers, but not the ORDER. You know the packet
    visited routers 0, 1, 2, and 3, but you don't know if it
    went 0->1->2->3 or 3->2->1->0. And in a big network with
    50 routers, you're sending 50 queries for every
    investigation, even though the attack path might only be
    3-4 routers long.


================================================================
  CHAPTER 4: OUR SOLUTION (What we built and why)
================================================================

We built THREE improvements, each one solving a specific
problem:

--- IMPROVEMENT 1: Replace Bloom with Cuckoo Filter ---
          (Solves: Evidence Destruction)

Instead of a Bloom Filter (which must wipe everything when
full), we use a Cuckoo Filter (which can delete individual
items). When the filter gets 90% full, our "Attack-Aware
Sliding Window" kicks in:

  1. Look at all stored entries.
  2. Find the oldest 10% of NORMAL (benign) traffic entries.
  3. Delete ONLY those benign entries.
  4. Attack entries? NEVER touched. Protected forever.

Result: The filter never fills up and never wipes. Attack
evidence survives indefinitely. In our experiments, this
achieved 96% traceability (vs Bloom's 2%). A 48x improvement.

But wait -- 96% is not 100%. Our professor asked: "Why is 4%
of evidence still lost?" The answer: during the cuckoo
"kicking" process, sometimes an attack entry gets
accidentally displaced. So we built Improvement 2...

--- IMPROVEMENT 2: Snapshot + Reset (Forensic Filter Manager) ---
          (Solves: 100% Traceability + Legal Compliance)

Instead of deleting ANYTHING, we use a completely different
approach:

  1. When the filter reaches 85% capacity (before any deletion
     could happen):
     a. SAVE the entire filter to disk as a permanent file
        (called a "snapshot").
     b. Lock that file so nobody can modify it (immutable).
     c. Create a brand new, empty filter for incoming traffic.

  2. When you need to look up an old packet:
     a. Check the current active filter first.
     b. If not found, check snapshot #1.
     c. If not found, check snapshot #2.
     d. Keep going through all snapshots.

  3. Nothing is EVER deleted. Every packet that was ever
     recorded still exists somewhere -- either in the active
     filter or in a snapshot on disk.

Result: 100% traceability. Zero deletions. Zero wipes. Fully
forensically sound (legal evidence standards met).

The trade-off: you use more DISK space (for snapshots). But
disk space is cheap. In our experiment, 5 snapshots used only
496 KB (about 0.5 MB). For context, a single smartphone photo
is 3-5 MB. So the cost is negligible.

--- IMPROVEMENT 3: Parent-Pointer Traceback ---
          (Solves: Slow and Unordered Traceback)

Every time a router forwards a packet, it remembers WHO gave
it that packet (the "parent" router). This information is
stored inside the Cuckoo Filter alongside the fingerprint.

To trace an attack path:

  Old method (SPIE): Ask ALL 50 routers "Did you see this?"
    -> 50 queries, get unordered set {R0, R1, R2, R3}
    -> Don't know the direction

  Our method (Parent-Pointer): Start at the victim's router.
    -> Ask R3: "Who gave you this?" Answer: R2.
    -> Ask R2: "Who gave you this?" Answer: R1.
    -> Ask R1: "Who gave you this?" Answer: R0.
    -> Ask R0: "Who gave you this?" Answer: Nobody (attacker
       entered here!).
    -> DONE. Path = R0 -> R1 -> R2 -> R3. Ordered. Only 4
       queries instead of 50.

This is 42% fewer queries in a 6-router network, and 17x
fewer queries in a 50-router network.


================================================================
  CHAPTER 5: THE NETWORK WE SIMULATED
================================================================

Since we don't have real network hardware, we simulated an
SDN network in Python code.

--- LINEAR TOPOLOGY (original) ---

  H0         H1       H2         H3
  |          |        |          |
  S0 -- S1 -- S2 -- S3 -- S4 -- S5

  H0 = Attacker's computer
  H3 = Victim's computer
  S0-S5 = 6 software switches (routers)

  The attacker (H0) sends malicious packets to the victim (H3).
  The packets travel through S0 -> S1 -> S2 -> S3.

--- TREE TOPOLOGY (new -- more realistic) ---

  Real networks don't look like straight lines. They look like
  trees:

                 S0 (Core Switch)
                /              \
        S1 (Aggregation)    S2 (Aggregation)
        /        \          /        \
    S3 (Access) S4 (Acc)  S5 (Acc) S6 (Access)
     |           |         |          |
    H0          H1        H2         H3
  (Attacker1) (Attacker2) (Victim2) (Victim1)

  This has 7 switches in 3 layers:
    - Core: The main backbone (S0)
    - Aggregation: Middle layer (S1, S2)
    - Access: Edge switches connecting to computers (S3-S6)

  We simulate TWO SIMULTANEOUS ATTACKS:
    Attack 1: H0 -> H3 (path: S3 -> S1 -> S0 -> S2 -> S6)
    Attack 2: H1 -> H2 (path: S4 -> S1 -> S0 -> S2 -> S5)

  Both attacks share the same core switches (S0, S1, S2) but
  diverge at the access layer. Our system correctly traces
  BOTH attack paths with 100% accuracy.


================================================================
  CHAPTER 6: THE EXPERIMENTS WE RAN
================================================================

We used the CIC-IDS2017 dataset: 50,000 real network flow
records from the Tuesday file. Of these, 44,807 are normal
(benign) traffic and 5,193 are FTP-Patator brute-force
attacks.

We tracked 100 specific attack packets and measured: after
processing all 50,000 packets, how many of those 100 can we
still find?

--- EXPERIMENT 1: Offline Filter Comparison ---

  Bloom Filter vs Cuckoo Filter, single filter, 50,000 packets.

  Result:
    Bloom Filter:  2 out of 100 attack packets found   (2%)
    Cuckoo Filter: 96 out of 100 attack packets found  (96%)

  The Bloom Filter wiped 4 times, destroying almost everything.
  The Cuckoo Filter never wiped -- it just deleted old benign
  traffic and kept all attacks.

--- EXPERIMENT 2: Network Traceback ---

  6-switch linear topology, 50,000 packets, 3 strategies.

  Results:
    Bloom + SPIE:           0/100 attacks traced  (0%)
    Cuckoo + SPIE:          100/100 traced        (100%)
    Cuckoo + Parent-Pointer: 97/100 traced        (97%)

  Bloom's 14 wipes across 6 switches destroyed everything.
  Cuckoo preserved all evidence. Parent-Pointer also recovered
  the ORDERED path in 79/100 cases.

--- EXPERIMENT 3: Scalability ---

  How does performance change with more switches?

    6 switches:  SPIE = 6 queries,  PP = 4 queries  (33% savings)
    10 switches: SPIE = 10 queries, PP = 4 queries  (60% savings)
    20 switches: SPIE = 20 queries, PP = 3 queries  (85% savings)
    50 switches: SPIE = 50 queries, PP = 3 queries  (94% savings)

  SPIE queries grow linearly. Parent-Pointer stays flat.
  Accuracy remains 99-100% at all sizes.

--- EXPERIMENT 4: Three-Way Comparison ---

  Bloom vs Cuckoo-Delete vs Snapshot+Reset, 50,000 packets.

  Results:
    Bloom:           2/100 traceability, 4 wipes
    Cuckoo-Delete:   96/100 traceability, 0 wipes, 41,000 deletions
    Snapshot+Reset:  100/100 traceability, 0 wipes, 0 deletions

  Snapshot+Reset wins on every metric. The cost? 496 KB of disk.

--- EXPERIMENT 5: Tree Topology + Multiple Attacks ---

  7-switch tree, two simultaneous attack types, Snapshot+Reset.

  Results:
    Bloom + SPIE:           1/100 traced
    Cuckoo + Parent-Pointer: 100/100 traced (both attack types)

--- EXPERIMENT 6: Fingerprint Size Sweep ---

  What if we make fingerprints bigger? More memory, but fewer
  false positives.

    8-bit  fingerprints: FPR = 3.13%,  RAM = 9.77 KB  (-20%)
    10-bit fingerprints: FPR = 0.78%,  RAM = 12.21 KB (baseline)
    12-bit fingerprints: FPR = 0.20%,  RAM = 14.65 KB (+20%)
    14-bit fingerprints: FPR = 0.05%,  RAM = 17.09 KB (+40%)
    16-bit fingerprints: FPR = 0.01%,  RAM = 19.53 KB (+60%)

  With Snapshot+Reset, ALL sizes achieve 100% traceability.
  Sweet spot: 12-bit (4x lower false positive rate for just
  20% more memory).


================================================================
  CHAPTER 7: THE CODE FILES (What each file does)
================================================================

Here is every file in our project and what it does:

--- bloom_filter.py ---
  The "old" approach. Implements a standard Bloom Filter.
  When it fills up, it WIPES everything. This is the BASELINE
  we compare against.

  Key parts:
  - Uses MurmurHash (mmh3) to generate hash positions
  - Uses a bitarray (row of 0s and 1s) as storage
  - insert(): hashes the item, flips bits ON
  - lookup(): checks if all the right bits are ON
  - When capacity is hit: WIPE (all bits back to 0)

--- cuckoo_filter.py ---
  Our improved filter. Implements a Cuckoo Filter with:
  - Fingerprint-based storage (10-bit fingerprints by default)
  - Two candidate bucket positions per item (cuckoo hashing)
  - Attack-aware sliding window (deletes benign, protects attacks)
  - Parent-pointer storage (which router sent the packet)
  - Victim stash (overflow for items that can't find a home)
  - Configurable fingerprint size (8, 10, 12, 14, or 16 bits)

  Key parts:
  - insert(): stores fingerprint + is_attack flag + parent ID
  - delete(): removes a specific fingerprint (impossible in Bloom!)
  - lookup(): checks both candidate positions
  - get_parent(): retrieves the parent-pointer for traceback
  - _sliding_window(): runs when 90% full, deletes oldest benign

--- forensic_filter_manager.py ---
  The "Snapshot + Reset" system. Wraps a Cuckoo Filter.
  - When filter reaches 85% capacity: serialize to disk, start new
  - lookup() searches active filter + all snapshots
  - ZERO deletions. ZERO wipes. 100% traceability.
  - Forensically sound (immutable evidence).

--- network_simulator.py ---
  Simulates a complete SDN network in Python:
  - SDNNetwork class: creates switches, hosts, topology
  - SoftSwitch class: each switch has its own Bloom + Cuckoo filter
  - SDNController class: runs forensic investigations
  - Supports both LINEAR and TREE topologies
  - Routes packets using BFS shortest path
  - Implements all 3 traceback strategies

--- analysis.py ---
  The offline experiment script:
  - Loads 50,000 CIC-IDS2017 records
  - Inserts into Bloom and Cuckoo filters simultaneously
  - Tracks 100 attack packets
  - Measures traceability, memory, FPR every 500 insertions
  - Produces results.png (4-panel comparison graph)

--- network_analysis.py ---
  The network experiment script:
  - Builds 6-switch SDN topology
  - Sends 50,000 packets through the network
  - Runs all 3 traceback strategies on 100 tracked attacks
  - Measures FPR/FNR, query efficiency
  - Runs scalability sweep (6, 10, 20, 50 switches)
  - Produces results_network.png and results_scalability.png

--- enhanced_analysis.py ---
  The "guide feedback" script (addresses all 6 points):
  - Step 1: Three-way comparison (Bloom vs Cuckoo vs Snapshot)
  - Step 2: Tree topology with multiple simultaneous attacks
  - Step 3: Five concrete use cases
  - Step 4: Fingerprint size sweep (cost-benefit analysis)
  - Produces results_enhanced.png (4-panel graph)

--- report.tex ---
  The LaTeX report (compile on Overleaf.com to get PDF).
  Contains all theory, methodology, results, and analysis.


================================================================
  CHAPTER 8: WHY THIS MATTERS (Real-World Use Cases)
================================================================

Use Case 1: POST-INCIDENT INVESTIGATION
  A bank gets hacked on Tuesday at 2 AM. The security team
  investigates on Thursday. With Bloom Filters, the logs were
  wiped on Wednesday. With our system, snapshots from Tuesday
  are safe on disk. Full investigation is possible.

Use Case 2: GDPR COMPLIANCE (European Privacy Law)
  A European user says "Delete my data!" (their legal right).
  Bloom Filter: impossible without deleting EVERYONE's data.
  Cuckoo Filter: delete() removes just that user's data.

Use Case 3: LONG-RUNNING ATTACKS (APT)
  A hacker slowly steals data over 30 days, hiding in normal
  traffic. Each day's filter is archived as a snapshot. When
  discovered on day 31, investigators can search all 30
  snapshots to reconstruct the entire 30-day attack.

Use Case 4: MULTI-TENANT CLOUD
  Amazon/Google run SDNs shared by thousands of companies.
  Company A gets attacked. The investigation must examine
  Company A's traffic without touching Company B's data.
  Attack-aware Cuckoo Filters keep them separated.

Use Case 5: DELAYED FORENSICS
  An IDS (Intrusion Detection System) flags an alert at 3 AM.
  The security analyst doesn't see it until 9 AM. By then,
  the Bloom Filter has wiped twice. With Snapshot+Reset, the
  3 AM snapshot is intact and the analyst can investigate.
